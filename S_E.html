Introduction** **Social engineering** is one of the most powerful and deceptive attack strategies used in cybersecurity breaches. Unlike technical hacking methods that exploit software or hardware vulnerabilities, **social engineering manipulates human psychology** to gain unauthorized access to systems, networks, or data. > üîç **Definition**: Social engineering is the art of manipulating people into performing actions or divulging confidential information. It often bypasses strong technical defenses by **tricking users into handing over access**, making it a favorite tool among hackers and cybercriminals. --- ## **2. Importance in Ethical Hacking** From an ethical hacker‚Äôs perspective, understanding social engineering is crucial because: * **Human error is the weakest link** in cybersecurity. * Over 90% of successful attacks begin with social engineering. * It helps ethical hackers test the **effectiveness of security awareness** and policies. --- ## **3. How Social Engineering Works** ### General Steps: 1. **Information Gathering**: Attacker collects details about the target (company name, employee names, emails, etc.). 2. **Relationship Building**: Builds trust or a sense of urgency (posing as HR, IT, or support). 3. **Exploitation**: Manipulates the victim to take an action (e.g., clicking a link, revealing credentials). 4. **Execution**: Gains access to systems or installs malware. 5. **Exit/Repeat**: Attacker either leaves no trace or repeats for further compromise. --- ## **4. Types of Social Engineering Attacks** | Type | Description | | ---------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------ | | **Phishing** | Fraudulent emails or messages that appear to come from a trusted source. | | **Spear Phishing** | Targeted phishing directed at specific individuals or organizations. | | **Vishing (Voice Phishing)** | Using phone calls to trick victims into giving away information. | | **Smishing (SMS Phishing)** | Using text messages to lure victims into clicking malicious links. | | **Pretexting** | Creating a fabricated scenario to extract information. | | **Baiting** | Luring users with attractive offers (like free music or USB drives). | | **Tailgating** | Physically following an authorized person into a restricted area. | | **Quid Pro Quo** | Offering a service in return for information (e.g., fake tech support). | | **Impersonation** | Pretending to be someone with authority (like IT staff or executives). | --- ## **5.
