Photometric Stereo

Introduction

Photometric Stereo is a powerful computer vision method used to reconstruct the 3D shape of a surface by observing it under different lighting conditions, but from a fixed camera viewpoint. Unlike Shape from Shading, which relies on a single image and assumes lighting direction, Photometric Stereo uses multiple images taken under known light directions, providing richer information for reconstructing the surface normals and depth map.

This technique is especially valuable in applications where high-precision surface modeling is required, such as cultural heritage preservation, biomedical imaging, and industrial inspection.

Basic Principle

The key idea behind Photometric Stereo is:

By illuminating an object from various known light sources and capturing the reflected intensities at each pixel, we can determine the orientation (normal vector) of the surface at that point.

Imaging Setup

  • Fixed Camera: Captures all images from the same viewpoint.
  • Variable Light Sources: Multiple directional lights are turned on one at a time.
  • Assumption of Lambertian Surface: The surface reflects light diffusely (same brightness when viewed from any angle).

Let’s say we take n images, each under a different known light direction L₁, L₂, ..., Lₙ.

For each pixel (x, y), the intensity I₁, I₂, ..., Iₙ in those images forms a system of equations:

I₁ = ρ * (N · L₁)
I₂ = ρ * (N · L₂)
...
Iₙ = ρ * (N · Lₙ)

Where:

  • I₁, I₂, ..., Iₙ are observed intensities at a pixel under different lights.
  • ρ is the surface albedo (reflectivity).
  • N is the unit surface normal vector.
  • L₁, L₂, ..., Lₙ are known lighting direction vectors.
  • (N · Lᵢ) is the dot product between normal and light direction.

By solving this system (usually via least squares when n ≥ 3), we estimate N and ρ.

Once normals (N) are known at every pixel, surface depth (z) can be estimated by integrating the gradient field derived from the normals.

Key Features

Feature Description
Multiple Images Needed Minimum 3 for solving 3D normals (X, Y, Z).
High Accuracy More robust and precise than single-image methods like Shape from Shading.
Albedo Recovery In addition to shape, the surface reflectance (ρ) can also be estimated.
Local Surface Estimation Produces a dense map of normals — one per pixel.
Non-Intrusive Uses visible light and passive capture (ideal for fragile artifacts, etc.).

Applications

  • Cultural Heritage: Capture fine details of ancient coins, sculptures, or inscriptions.
  • Medical Imaging: Model the surface of skin, organs, or tissue samples.
  • Industrial Inspection: Detect microscopic surface defects like dents, scratches, or pits.
  • 3D Scanning: Augment or replace laser-based scanning in certain applications.
  • Biometrics: Fingerprint and palmprint surface capture with enhanced depth cues.

Challenges

Challenge Explanation
Non-Lambertian Materials Reflective or translucent surfaces distort intensity measurements.
Shadowing and Interreflections Some areas might be poorly lit or receive bounced light from other surfaces.
Lighting Calibration Inaccurate light direction measurements lead to incorrect normal estimation.
Integration of Normals Reconstructing depth from normals is mathematically complex and error-prone.
Specular Highlights Bright reflections violate the Lambertian assumption and affect normal calculation.

Comparison with Shape from Shading

Feature Shape from Shading Photometric Stereo
Number of Images One Multiple (≥ 3)
Camera Setup Static Static
Light Source Single (assumed) Multiple (known)
Accuracy Low to Moderate High
Complexity Simpler Moderate to Complex
Surface Type Support Smooth, Lambertian Lambertian preferred
Depth Estimation Indirect, ambiguous More direct and accurate

Conclusion

Photometric Stereo is a robust and elegant approach for extracting fine-grained 3D surface information by utilizing multiple lighting directions with a static camera. It bypasses the inherent ambiguity of single-image techniques and offers pixel-wise surface normal and reflectance estimation. While it assumes ideal lighting and surface properties, modern adaptations using deep learning and multi-view integration are making Photometric Stereo more versatile and powerful than ever.
