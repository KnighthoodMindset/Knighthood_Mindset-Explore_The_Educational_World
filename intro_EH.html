## üåê **Introduction to Ethical Hacking** --- ### üîç **What is Ethical Hacking?** **Ethical Hacking** refers to the practice of deliberately probing systems, networks, and applications for security vulnerabilities with the permission of the owner. The goal is to identify weaknesses before malicious hackers can exploit them. Ethical hackers, often called **white-hat hackers**, use the same techniques as malicious hackers but in a controlled, legal, and constructive manner to enhance system security. Unlike black-hat hackers, who break the law for personal or financial gain, **ethical hackers** work within **legal and ethical boundaries** to identify vulnerabilities, assess security defenses, and protect the system from potential breaches. --- ### üï∞Ô∏è **History of Ethical Hacking** | Year | Milestone | | --------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | | **1960s-70s** | Initially, hackers were seen as enthusiasts who were skilled at solving problems and tinkering with technology at institutions like MIT. This process helps ethical hackers identify vulnerabilities and provide recommendations for securing systems. Here‚Äôs a **detailed breakdown** of how it works: #### 1. **Planning and Reconnaissance** The first step in ethical hacking is **preparation**. This is when the ethical hacker interacts with the client or the organization to understand the scope of the engagement and obtain permission to conduct the test. * **Define Scope**: The ethical hacker and the organization agree on the scope of the test, such as which systems, networks, or applications will be tested. This step helps avoid any misunderstandings about what is allowed. * **Reconnaissance**: Ethical hackers gather as much information as possible about the target system, often referred to as **OSINT** (Open Source Intelligence). This includes: * **Passive Reconnaissance**: Gathering information without direct interaction with the target, such as WHOIS queries, DNS lookups, social media, and public records. * **Active Reconnaissance**: Directly probing the target with tools like **ping scans**, **network sweeps**, and **port scans** to gather information on active systems, open ports, and services running. #### 2. **Scanning and Enumeration** Once enough information has been gathered, ethical hackers begin the **scanning** phase. This involves mapping the target systems to identify potential weaknesses. * **Network Scanning**: Tools like **Nmap** and **Nessus** are used to discover open ports, running services, and potential entry points. * **Vulnerability Scanning**: Automated tools scan the systems for known vulnerabilities. These tools can match the target systems against vulnerability databases like **CVE** (Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures). * **Enumeration**: This step involves **interacting with the identified services** to extract detailed information, such as banner grabbing and identifying possible login points for further attacks. #### 3. **Gaining Access** In this phase, ethical hackers attempt to exploit vulnerabilities to gain access to the system. This step is crucial because it simulates how an attacker might enter the system. * **Exploitation**: Ethical hackers use **exploit tools** like **Metasploit** to actively attempt attacks such as SQL injection, buffer overflows, or exploiting weak credentials. * **Social Engineering**: In some cases, ethical hackers may simulate **phishing** or **social engineering** attacks to test human factors like employees falling for fake emails or phone calls designed to gain access to systems. #### 4. **Maintaining Access** Once access has been gained, ethical hackers may attempt to **maintain a foothold** within the target system. This step is useful for demonstrating how an attacker might establish a **long-term presence**. * **Backdoors**: The ethical hacker may install backdoors to show how easily an attacker could come back later to exploit the system. * **Privilege Escalation**: Ethical hackers try to escalate their privileges to the highest level of system access (e.g., root/admin) to gain full control. #### 5. **Analysis and Reporting** After completing the penetration test, ethical hackers analyze the findings and compile a detailed report. This report includes the vulnerabilities identified, the severity of the risks, and recommendations for mitigating or eliminating the issues. * **Report**: The final report documents every step of the engagement, from reconnaissance to exploitation and findings, along with actionable recommendations. It may also suggest implementing **patch management**, **firewall configurations**, or **multi-factor authentication** to enhance security. --- ### üîë **Key Features of Ethical Hacking** | Feature |
