Types of Sniffing

Sniffing can be broadly classified based on the method and intent used to capture data on a network. Understanding these types helps ethical hackers and security professionals recognize threats and implement appropriate defenses.

1. Passive Sniffing

Definition:

Passive sniffing refers to the act of monitoring network traffic without actively interfering with the network or its components. It is a silent form of surveillance.

How It Works:
  • The attacker’s Network Interface Card (NIC) is placed in promiscuous mode.
  • The attacker captures and analyzes packets that are broadcasted in the network.
  • This method works primarily in hub-based networks, where data is sent to all nodes.
Key Features:
FeatureDescription
Non-intrusiveNo modification or interaction with data traffic.
StealthyHard to detect due to lack of network activity.
Limited ScopeOnly captures broadcast traffic or traffic to/from the sniffer's device.
Example Scenario:

An attacker in a public Wi-Fi café uses Wireshark to capture unsecured HTTP traffic and extract login credentials from websites.

Usage:
  • Legitimate use in network troubleshooting.
  • Malicious use in eavesdropping on sensitive data.

2. Active Sniffing

Definition:

Active sniffing involves injecting packets or modifying network behavior to redirect traffic toward the attacker’s system. It is more intrusive than passive sniffing.

How It Works:
  • Used in switched networks, where traffic is directed only to the destination device.
  • The attacker uses techniques such as ARP poisoning, MAC flooding, or DNS spoofing to mislead the network switch and capture traffic.
Key Features:
FeatureDescription
IntrusiveAlters normal network behavior.
DetectableCan be detected through abnormal traffic or alerts.
Broader AccessCan capture targeted traffic not normally visible.
Common Techniques in Active Sniffing:
TechniqueDescription
ARP PoisoningSpoofs ARP tables to redirect traffic through the attacker’s device.
MAC FloodingOverloads switch MAC table to force it into hub-like behavior.
DNS SpoofingSends fake DNS responses to redirect users to malicious websites.
MITM AttacksIntercepts communication between two devices for analysis or manipulation.
Example Scenario:

An attacker inside a corporate network performs ARP spoofing to intercept communication between an employee and the company’s intranet.

3. Protocol-based Sniffing

This refers to sniffing targeted at specific network protocols. Certain protocols are more vulnerable due to their lack of encryption.

Examples:
ProtocolSniffing Risk
HTTPCaptures usernames, passwords, and form data in plain text.
FTPTransfers files and credentials unencrypted.
SMTP/POP3Exposes email content and credentials.
TelnetTransmits commands and login data in cleartext.

4. Application-level Sniffing

Definition:

This type of sniffing targets specific applications or services (e.g., email clients, chat messengers, web apps) to capture sensitive data.

Example Tools:
  • Dsniff: Captures credentials from FTP, HTTP, POP, etc.
  • Ettercap: Performs advanced sniffing with plugin support.

5. Wireless Sniffing

Definition:

Wireless sniffing involves capturing packets from Wi-Fi networks. This can include both encrypted and unencrypted traffic.

Types:
  • Passive Wireless Sniffing: Captures beacons and probe requests from nearby devices.
  • Active Wireless Sniffing: Involves sending deauthentication packets to force devices to reconnect, capturing handshake packets.
Tools Used:
  • Kismet
  • Aircrack-ng
  • Wireshark (with wireless interface)

Comparison Table: Passive vs Active Sniffing

FeaturePassive SniffingActive Sniffing
Network TypeHub-based networksSwitched networks
IntrusivenessNon-intrusiveIntrusive
DetectabilityHard to detectEasier to detect
Attack TechniquesNoneARP spoofing, MAC flooding
Use CasesMonitoring, troubleshootingTargeted attacks, redirection

6. Ethical Use of Sniffing

While sniffing is often associated with malicious intent, it also has legitimate uses in ethical hacking:

  • Network administrators use sniffers to troubleshoot connectivity issues and optimize performance.
  • Ethical hackers use sniffing during penetration tests to find vulnerabilities.
  • Security researchers analyze suspicious traffic and malware behavior.

Important: Always obtain permission before conducting any form of sniffing to avoid legal or ethical violations.

Conclusion

Understanding the different types of sniffing helps both attackers and defenders recognize the scope of risk and the right defenses to employ. Passive sniffing is stealthy but limited in scope, while active sniffing is powerful but risky. Ethical hackers must master these techniques to secure networks against unauthorized access and data leakage.
