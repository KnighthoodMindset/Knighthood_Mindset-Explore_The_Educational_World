In a **binary image**, the function is typically applied to the **foreground pixels** (object pixels) and calculates their distance from the **nearest background pixel**, or vice versa. The most common procedure used is called the **Distance Transform**, which converts a binary image into a grayscale image where each pixel‚Äôs value represents the distance to the nearest zero (background) pixel. #### üîÑ Steps of Distance Transform: 1. **Input**: A binary image with foreground and background pixels. 2. **Labeling**: Identify all background pixels (usually 0) and mark foreground pixels (usually 1). 3. **Transformation**: For each foreground pixel, calculate the distance to the nearest background pixel using a distance metric. 4. **Output**: A grayscale image where brighter pixels indicate larger distances from the background. --- ### üìè **Common Distance Metrics** Different types of distance functions are used depending on the desired precision and context: 1. **Euclidean Distance** (L2 Norm): $$ D = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2} $$ * Gives the **straight-line** distance between two points. * Most accurate and isotropic but computationally expensive. 2. **Manhattan Distance** (L1 Norm): $$ D = |x_2 - x_1| + |y_2 - y_1| $$ * Measures the distance along axes like moving on a grid. * Faster to compute, but less natural for curved shapes. 3. **Chessboard Distance** (L‚àû Norm): $$ D = \max(|x_2 - x_1|, |y_2 - y_1|) $$ * Measures the number of moves a king would take in chess. * Useful for block-based or morphological operations. 4. **City Block Distance** (special case of Manhattan Distance): * Often used in discrete grid-based images. * Approximates real-world travel distances in urban layouts.
