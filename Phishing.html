Introduction** **Phishing** is a deceptive cyberattack where attackers impersonate trustworthy entities to **steal sensitive information** such as login credentials, credit card numbers, personal identification details, or financial data. It is one of the **most common and successful social engineering attacks** due to its simplicity, scalability, and effectiveness. Phishing attacks exploit **human psychology**‚Äîcuriosity, fear, urgency, and trust‚Äîto trick victims into clicking malicious links or opening infected attachments. --- ## **2. Importance in Ethical Hacking** Understanding the **phishing process** is essential for ethical hackers to: * Simulate phishing attacks in penetration tests. * Evaluate an organization's resilience against social engineering threats. * Train employees to recognize and avoid phishing. * Help develop countermeasures to detect and prevent attacks. --- ## **3. Phishing Lifecycle / Process** The phishing process typically consists of **seven structured phases**, from planning the attack to stealing information and exploiting it. --- ### **Phase 1: Information Gathering / Target Selection** In this phase, the attacker identifies and studies the target (individual or organization) using: * Publicly available data (social media, LinkedIn, company websites). * Leaked data from previous breaches. * Search engines and people-finder websites. * Tools like Maltego, Recon-ng, and Google Dorking. **Objective**: Gather enough personal and organizational details to craft a **believable phishing message**. --- ### **Phase 2: Crafting the Attack Message** The attacker creates a **convincing email, SMS, or webpage** that impersonates a trusted entity like: * A bank * A colleague * A government agency * A popular service (Google, Microsoft, Facebook) **Tactics used**: * Urgent language: ‚ÄúYour account will be suspended!‚Äù * Lures: ‚ÄúClaim your reward now!‚Äù * Fear: ‚ÄúUnusual activity detected!‚Äù This message typically includes: * A **malicious URL** pointing to a fake login page. * A **malicious attachment** (e.g., Word doc, Excel file, PDF). * A **call to action** encouraging users to click or download. --- ### **Phase 3: Delivery of the Phishing Message** The message is sent to the target through different vectors: | **Medium** | **Technique Name** | **Example** | | ------------ | ------------------ | ---------------------------------------------- | | Email | Phishing | ‚ÄúReset your Gmail password now!‚Äù | | SMS | Smishing | ‚ÄúYour bank account is locked, click here.‚Äù | | Phone call | Vishing | ‚ÄúWe‚Äôre calling from your credit card company.‚Äù | | Social Media | Phishing | Fake messages or links from friends | | Ads/Websites | Malvertising | Fake antivirus warnings or tech support | Attackers may use **email spoofing** or compromised domains to make the message seem legitimate. --- ### **Phase 4: Exploitation** Once the target **clicks the link or opens the attachment**, the actual phishing attack is executed. **Two common scenarios:** 1. **Credential Harvesting** * The link leads to a fake website mimicking a legitimate login page. * Victim enters credentials. * Credentials are sent to the attacker. 2. **Malware Delivery** * The attachment contains malware (e.g., keylogger, ransomware). * Opening the file activates the malicious payload. In both cases, the attacker silently gains **unauthorized access** to the victim‚Äôs system or accounts. --- ### **Phase 5: Data Collection** Once credentials or other data are captured, they are stored in an attacker-controlled location (e.g., a server, dark web repository). In malware-based attacks, the malware might continuously send: * Keystrokes * Screenshots * Passwords * System data --- ### **Phase 6: Exploitation of Stolen Information** Attackers use the harvested data for malicious purposes such as: * Identity theft * Unauthorized bank transactions * Selling credentials on the dark web * Internal network compromise (if credentials are reused in corporate systems) In more complex attacks, phishing is the **first step in a multi-stage attack**, leading to lateral movement inside a network. --- ### **Phase 7: Covering Tracks** Attackers may: * Remove traces of the phishing page or malware. * Use proxies or VPNs to hide their location. * Automatically redirect phishing URLs to legitimate sites after the attack to avoid suspicion. --- ## **4.
