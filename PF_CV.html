In computer vision, particle filters are extensively used for **object tracking**, **robot localization**, and **motion estimation**. They work by representing the probability distribution of a system‚Äôs state using a **set of random samples** (particles) and updating them over time based on observed data. Particle filters are a type of **Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC)** method and are especially useful when traditional filtering techniques like Kalman filters fail due to system complexity. --- ### ‚öôÔ∏è **How It Works:** The particle filter operates in a recursive loop involving the following steps: #### üü° 1. **Initialization:** * A set of `N` particles is generated. * Each particle represents a possible state of the system (e.g., position, velocity of an object). * Initially, particles are spread randomly over the possible state space. #### üü¢ 2. **Prediction (Motion Model):** * Each 3. **Update (Measurement Model):** * Each particle is assigned a **weight** based on how well it matches the current observation (e.g., image frame). * The weight reflects the **likelihood** of the observed data given the particle's state. #### üî¥ 4. **Resampling:** * Particles with higher weights are **duplicated**, while those with low weights are **discarded**. * This focuses computation on the most probable states. * Resampling avoids particle degeneracy (where most particles have negligible weight). #### üîÅ 5. **Loop:** * Steps 2‚Äì4 repeat for each new time step or observation. --- ### üìä **Mathematical Foundation:** The state estimation at time `t`, given observations up to `t`, is computed using Bayes' theorem: $$ p(x_t | z_{1:t}) \propto p(z_t | x_t) \int p(x_t | x_{t-1}) **Robustness:** Handles noise and occlusion in vision-based applications. --- ### ‚úÖ **Advantages:** 1. **Handles Complex Models:** Unlike Kalman filters, particle filters work with non-linear and
