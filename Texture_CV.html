Texture

Introduction

In our everyday perception, we can distinguish between surfaces like grass, fabric, sand, or bricks — not just by color or shape, but because of a property known as texture. In the field of computer vision, texture plays a vital role in interpreting images that contain patterns or surface details.

Texture refers to the visual patterns of spatial variations in intensity or color, typically repeating over a surface. It is used to describe the surface quality of objects and scenes. Unlike edges or corners, which are localized features, texture represents spatial relationships over a region — offering crucial information for segmentation, recognition, and classification.

What is Texture?

In simple terms:

Texture is the feel or appearance of a surface or substance in an image, reflected by repeating patterns or intensity variations.

It is not defined at a single point, but is rather a property of a region. Texture does not have precise boundaries, but it contains repetitive or stochastic patterns of intensity.

Characteristics of Texture:
  • Regularity or randomness
  • Coarseness or smoothness
  • Directionality (orientation)
  • Contrast
  • Frequency of pattern

Types of Texture

1. Structural Texture
  • Made up of basic primitive patterns (like lines, dots, or shapes) arranged in a repetitive or rule-based manner.
  • Examples: brick walls, woven fabric
2. Statistical Texture
  • More random in appearance, lacking precise structure.
  • Texture is described by statistical measures of intensity variation.
  • Examples: grass, clouds, sand

Why Texture Matters in Computer Vision

Texture provides valuable cues for:

  • Segmentation: Dividing an image into textured regions (e.g., separating land types in satellite imagery)
  • Classification: Identifying material types (e.g., cloth vs. metal)
  • Recognition: Recognizing faces, fingerprints, skin diseases, etc.
  • Object detection: Enhancing accuracy where shape or color is insufficient

In many real-world images where edges or contours are weak or missing, texture becomes the primary visual clue.

Texture Analysis Methods

Texture can be analyzed using three major approaches:

1. Statistical Methods

These methods describe texture based on the distribution of pixel intensities in a region.

a. Gray Level Co-occurrence Matrix (GLCM)
  • Measures how often pairs of pixels with specific values and a specific spatial relationship occur.
  • Features derived from GLCM:
    • Contrast
    • Correlation
    • Energy (Angular Second Moment)
    • Homogeneity

For example, in a GLCM, we compute how often pixel value i is next to value j in a given direction and distance.

b. Local Binary Pattern (LBP)
  • Compares each pixel to its neighbors to form a binary number.
  • Very effective for texture classification and face recognition.
c. Statistical Moments
  • Uses mean, variance, skewness, kurtosis of pixel intensities to describe texture.

2. Structural Methods

Assume that texture is composed of basic patterns (called texels) repeated in a specific arrangement.

  • Detect and analyze primitive shapes (e.g., lines, circles, etc.)
  • Analyze arrangement (e.g., grid-like, random, parallel)

Suitable for: Highly organized textures like fabric, tiles, architecture

3. Transform-Based Methods

These methods analyze texture in frequency or scale-space domains.

a. Fourier Transform
  • Measures frequency content of an image.
  • Detects repetitive patterns and orientation.
b. Wavelet Transform
  • Analyzes texture at multiple resolutions (fine and coarse).
  • Ideal for hierarchical textures like in nature or medical images.
c. Gabor Filters
  • Apply orientation-sensitive and frequency-sensitive filters.
  • Mimic human visual perception.
  • Widely used in face recognition and defect detection.

Applications of Texture in Computer Vision

  • Medical Imaging: Classifying tissues, tumors, skin conditions
  • Remote Sensing: Land cover classification (urban, forest, water)
  • Industrial Inspection: Surface defect detection (scratches, dents)
  • Face Recognition: Using LBP or Gabor filters for robust detection
  • Textile Industry: Identifying cloth textures, pattern matching
  • Animal Identification: Texture-based species recognition (fur, scales)
  • Object Classification: Differentiating materials (wood, glass, metal)

Texture vs Color vs Shape

Feature Description Suitable For
Color Pixel intensity in RGB/HSV Simple, color-based classification
Shape Contours, edges Object boundary detection
Texture Surface pattern across regions Material identification, surfaces

Texture is often used in combination with other features (like shape and color) for a more holistic understanding.

Challenges in Texture Analysis

  • Noise Sensitivity: Texture descriptors can be disrupted by noise.
  • Scale and Orientation Variation: Patterns may appear differently under zoom or rotation.
  • Illumination Dependence: Shadows or lighting changes affect texture visibility.
  • Complexity in Natural Images: Real-world scenes contain mixed and irregular textures.

Modern solutions involve texture-invariant models or combining texture with deep learning techniques.

Texture and Deep Learning

While traditional texture analysis is rule-based, deep learning allows automatic feature extraction from raw pixels.

  • CNNs (Convolutional Neural Networks) can learn texture patterns through layers.
  • Pre-trained models like ResNet or VGG capture texture as part of object recognition.
  • Style Transfer in neural art generation also manipulates texture explicitly.

Still, in many industrial and medical systems, classical texture analysis remains in use for its interpretability and speed.

Conclusion

Texture is a rich and informative visual cue that allows machines to go beyond shape and color and understand the fine details of surfaces and materials. By using statistical, structural, or transform-based methods, texture can be effectively analyzed and used in a wide range of applications — from satellite images to skin lesion diagnosis.

Even in the era of deep learning, texture remains a foundational concept in computer vision — essential for segmentation, classification, and pattern recognition. Understanding how texture works gives students and developers a sharper insight into how machines perceive the real world.
