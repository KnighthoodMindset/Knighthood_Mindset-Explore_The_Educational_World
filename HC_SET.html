Introduction** **Social Engineering** is a psychological manipulation technique used by attackers to trick individuals into divulging confidential information or performing actions that compromise security. Instead of breaking through a firewall or exploiting software vulnerabilities, social engineers **exploit human behavior** such as trust, fear, urgency, curiosity, or greed. These attacks are categorized broadly into: * **Human-Based Social Engineering Techniques** * **Computer-Based Social Engineering Techniques** Both types aim to **bypass technical defenses** by attacking the weakest link in the security chain ‚Äî **the human**. --- ## **2. Importance in Ethical Hacking** Ethical hackers study social engineering to: * Simulate real-world attack scenarios during penetration testing. * Train organizations in detecting and mitigating manipulation. * Raise awareness about common deceptive techniques. Understanding both **human** and **computer-based techniques** is crucial to prevent identity theft, phishing, data breaches, and insider threats. --- ## **3. Human-Based Social Engineering Techniques** These involve **direct interaction between the attacker and the victim**, where the attacker manipulates the victim using psychological strategies. ### **3.1 Impersonation** The attacker pretends to be someone trustworthy ‚Äî like an IT technician, government official, or co-worker ‚Äî to extract confidential information. > *Example*: An attacker pretending to be tech support asking for login credentials. --- ### **3.2 Tailgating (Piggybacking)** The attacker physically follows an authorized person into a restricted area without providing credentials. > *Example*: Someone holding a coffee cup and pretending to forget their access card, asking you to hold the door. --- ### **3.3 Shoulder Surfing** Observing a user typing passwords or PINs by looking over their shoulder or using hidden cameras. > *Common in*: ATMs, internet caf√©s, or office desks. --- ### **3.4 Eavesdropping** Listening to private conversations to gather sensitive information such as usernames, passwords, or project details. --- ### **3.5 Pretexting** The attacker creates a fabricated scenario (pretext) to steal information or gain access. > *Example*: Calling an employee pretending to be an auditor requesting employee records. --- ### **3.6 Dumpster Diving** The attacker searches through physical trash bins to find sensitive documents like old passwords, confidential reports, or bills. --- ### **3.7 Baiting** Leaving a physical device (e.g., USB drive labeled "Salary Report 2025") in a visible place, expecting someone to plug it in and install malware. --- ### **3.8 Quid Pro Quo** The attacker offers something valuable (like technical support or free software) in exchange for confidential information. --- ### **Comparison Table ‚Äì Computer-Based Social Engineering Techniques** These techniques involve the use of **software, websites, or digital platforms** to manipulate users into compromising security. ### **4.1 Phishing** A fraudulent attempt to obtain sensitive information via fake emails, websites, or messages that appear to come from legitimate sources. > *Example*: A fake email from your bank asking you to verify your account by clicking a malicious link. --- ### **4.2 Spear Phishing** A **targeted phishing attack** aimed at a specific individual or organization, usually with personalized content. > *Example*: An email addressing you by name and mentioning your workplace to gain credibility. --- ### **4.3 Whaling** Spear phishing directed specifically at high-profile targets like CEOs, CFOs, or board members. > *Goal*: Gain access to corporate secrets or authorize financial transfers. --- ### **4.4 Vishing (Voice Phishing)** Using phone calls to deceive individuals into revealing sensitive data, like banking credentials or verification codes. > *Example*: Automated voice calls warning of suspicious account activity. --- ### **4.5 Smishing (SMS Phishing)** Sending malicious links or misleading messages via SMS to trick users into clicking on them. --- ### **4.6 Fake Software Updates** Creating pop-ups or websites that imitate software update notifications, tricking users into installing malware. --- ### **4.7 Rogue Security Software** Convincing users their system is infected and offering fake antivirus software that is actually malware. --- ### **4.8 Malicious Advertisements (Malvertising)** Embedding malware into ads on legitimate websites. Clicking on the ad may download a trojan or redirect to phishing sites. --- ### **Comparison Table ‚Äì Computer-Based Techniques** | Technique | Description | Platform | | -------------- | --------------------------------------- | --------------- | | Phishing | Fake emails or websites | Email, Web | | Vishing | Fake voice calls | Telephone | | Smishing | Malicious messages via SMS | Mobile | | Whaling | Attacks on high-level executives | Email, Phone | | Fake Updates | Malware disguised as software updates | Web Browser | | Rogue Software | Malware disguised as antivirus software | Desktop, Mobile | --- ## **5.
