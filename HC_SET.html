Human and Computer-Based Social Engineering Techniques

1. Introduction

Social Engineering is a psychological manipulation technique used by attackers to trick individuals into divulging confidential information or performing actions that compromise security. Instead of breaking through a firewall or exploiting software vulnerabilities, social engineers exploit human behavior such as trust, fear, urgency, curiosity, or greed.

These attacks are categorized broadly into:

  • Human-Based Social Engineering Techniques
  • Computer-Based Social Engineering Techniques

Both types aim to bypass technical defenses by attacking the weakest link in the security chain — the human.

2. Importance in Ethical Hacking

  • Simulate real-world attack scenarios during penetration testing.
  • Train organizations in detecting and mitigating manipulation.
  • Raise awareness about common deceptive techniques.

Understanding both human and computer-based techniques is crucial to prevent identity theft, phishing, data breaches, and insider threats.

3. Human-Based Social Engineering Techniques

These involve direct interaction between the attacker and the victim, where the attacker manipulates the victim using psychological strategies.

3.1 Impersonation

The attacker pretends to be someone trustworthy — like an IT technician, government official, or co-worker — to extract confidential information.

Example: An attacker pretending to be tech support asking for login credentials.
3.2 Tailgating (Piggybacking)

The attacker physically follows an authorized person into a restricted area without providing credentials.

Example: Someone holding a coffee cup and pretending to forget their access card, asking you to hold the door.
3.3 Shoulder Surfing

Observing a user typing passwords or PINs by looking over their shoulder or using hidden cameras.

Common in: ATMs, internet cafés, or office desks.

3.4 Eavesdropping

Listening to private conversations to gather sensitive information such as usernames, passwords, or project details.

3.5 Pretexting

The attacker creates a fabricated scenario (pretext) to steal information or gain access.

Example: Calling an employee pretending to be an auditor requesting employee records.
3.6 Dumpster Diving

The attacker searches through physical trash bins to find sensitive documents like old passwords, confidential reports, or bills.

3.7 Baiting

Leaving a physical device (e.g., USB drive labeled "Salary Report 2025") in a visible place, expecting someone to plug it in and install malware.

3.8 Quid Pro Quo

The attacker offers something valuable (like technical support or free software) in exchange for confidential information.

Comparison Table – Human-Based Techniques
Technique Description Attack Vector
Impersonation Pretending to be someone trusted Phone, Email, In-Person
Tailgating Gaining physical access via social compliance Building/Office
Shoulder Surfing Watching user input Physical Proximity
Pretexting Fake scenarios to gain trust Phone, Email
Dumpster Diving Searching physical waste Physical
Baiting Leaving infected physical devices USB, CDs

4. Computer-Based Social Engineering Techniques

These techniques involve the use of software, websites, or digital platforms to manipulate users into compromising security.

4.1 Phishing

A fraudulent attempt to obtain sensitive information via fake emails, websites, or messages that appear to come from legitimate sources.

Example: A fake email from your bank asking you to verify your account by clicking a malicious link.
4.2 Spear Phishing

A targeted phishing attack aimed at a specific individual or organization, usually with personalized content.

Example: An email addressing you by name and mentioning your workplace to gain credibility.
4.3 Whaling

Spear phishing directed specifically at high-profile targets like CEOs, CFOs, or board members.

Goal: Gain access to corporate secrets or authorize financial transfers.

4.4 Vishing (Voice Phishing)

Using phone calls to deceive individuals into revealing sensitive data, like banking credentials or verification codes.

Example: Automated voice calls warning of suspicious account activity.
4.5 Smishing (SMS Phishing)

Sending malicious links or misleading messages via SMS to trick users into clicking on them.

4.6 Fake Software Updates

Creating pop-ups or websites that imitate software update notifications, tricking users into installing malware.

4.7 Rogue Security Software

Convincing users their system is infected and offering fake antivirus software that is actually malware.

4.8 Malicious Advertisements (Malvertising)

Embedding malware into ads on legitimate websites. Clicking on the ad may download a trojan or redirect to phishing sites.

Comparison Table – Computer-Based Techniques
Technique Description Platform
Phishing Fake emails or websites Email, Web
Vishing Fake voice calls Telephone
Smishing Malicious messages via SMS Mobile
Whaling Attacks on high-level executives Email, Phone
Fake Updates Malware disguised as software updates Web Browser
Rogue Software Malware disguised as antivirus software Desktop, Mobile

5. Key Differences Between Human and Computer-Based Techniques

Aspect Human-Based Techniques Computer-Based Techniques
Method of Interaction Physical or social interactions Digital interactions
Tools Used Pretexting, impersonation, baiting Emails, pop-ups, fake apps
Detection Difficulty May be obvious in person Often hidden in software/web pages
Victim Awareness May suspect the intent Usually unaware until damage is done
Speed of Execution Slower, requires setup Faster, automated

6. Prevention and Defense Mechanisms

Applicable To: Both
  • Security Awareness Training

    Train employees to recognize manipulation attempts.

Applicable To: Computer-Based
  • Email Filtering Tools

    Block spam and phishing emails automatically.

  • Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

    Adds extra security even if credentials are leaked.

  • Regular Software Updates

    Patch vulnerabilities that can be exploited.

Applicable To: Human-Based
  • Caller ID Verification

    Detect spoofed numbers during voice phishing.

  • Access Control

    Restrict physical access to sensitive areas.

  • USB Port Control

    Prevents baiting via unknown USB devices.

7. Role of Ethical Hackers

  • Test organizational preparedness.
  • Educate staff on identifying and reporting such attempts.
  • Evaluate physical and digital security.
  • Conduct red teaming exercises combining both human and digital vectors.

8. Conclusion

Both human and computer-based social engineering techniques are powerful tools in a hacker's arsenal, exploiting human psychology more than system flaws. The line between these two is increasingly blurred as attackers blend them for advanced threats.

"Security is not just about systems; it's about people. And people must be trained, tested, and trusted."

Combating these attacks requires a multi-layered approach combining technology, awareness, policy enforcement, and ethical testing.
