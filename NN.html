Nearest Neighbours

What is Nearest Neighbours

Nearest Neighboursis a method used to classify or predict data based on the idea of "similarity". If you’ve ever chosen a movie to watch based on what’s similar to ones you've liked before — that's the core concept.

At its core, the Nearest Neighbours approach says:

If something looks similar to things we’ve already seen, it probably belongs in the same category.

How Does It Work

Imagine you're at a party, and you don’t know anyone. You spot someone wearing a band t-shirt of your favorite music group and chatting about a show you like. You’d probably guess you’d get along with them. This is how the algorithm thinks.

In Nearest Neighbours:

We already have examples of items (these could be emails, photos, patients, or customers).

We are given a new item (that we don't know much about).

The algorithm looks at the most similar items (the "nearest neighbours").

Then it makes a decision based on those similar items.

What Does “Neighbour” Mean in This Context?

A neighbour is just something that is similar to something else.

If you’re looking at a document, its neighbours could be other documents with similar words.

If you’re analyzing images, its neighbours could be images with similar patterns or colors.

The term "nearest" refers to how close or similar things are, not physically, but in terms of features or characteristics.

Key Concepts

No Training: This method doesn't need to "learn" in the usual sense. It just stores all the existing examples and makes decisions at the time of prediction.

Instance-Based Learning: It learns from the examples directly.

Lazy Learning: It delays the actual work until it needs to make a prediction.

Types

There are mainly two ways Nearest Neighbours is used:

  • K-Nearest Neighbours (KNN) – For classification and regression tasks.

    • Content-Based Recommendation – Finds similar items (e.g., books, music, videos).

    Applications

    1. Spam Detection:Checks whether is this email similar to spam emails?

    3. Medical Diagnosis:Does this patient have a condition similar to others?

    4. Image Recognition:Checks Which known image is this new image most similar to?

    Real World Analogy

    Think of Nearest Neighbours like this:

    You move to a new city and want to find a good pizza place. You ask five locals who live near you — they all recommend the same spot. You’ll likely try that place.

    Those five locals are your nearest neighbours, and you relied on their experience to make a decision.
