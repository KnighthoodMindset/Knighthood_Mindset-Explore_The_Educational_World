Sampling Distribution of an Estimator

In Statistical Learning, when we estimate a quantity (like the mean, variance, or prediction accuracy) based on a sample of data, the estimate will vary depending on which data points we happened to get.

This leads to the idea of a sampling distribution

The sampling distribution of an estimator is the probability distribution of the estimator when repeated samples are drawn from the population.

In simple words:

  • If you repeat the experiment many times (collect new samples each time),
  • calculate the estimate each time (like mean, variance, accuracy),
  • the pattern of all these estimates forms a sampling distribution.

    • Why Sampling Distribution Matters

    An estimator is random because it depends on the random sample.

    The sampling distribution tells us how much the estimator might vary from sample to sample.

    It helps in:

  • Measuring the accuracy of an estimate.
  • Constructing confidence intervals.
  • Performing hypothesis testing

    • Important Properties of Sampling Distribution

    Mean of Sampling Distribution:If the estimator is unbiased, the mean of the sampling distribution equals the true parameter.

    Variance of Sampling Distribution:Indicates how much the estimator varies between samples. Smaller variance means more reliable estimates.

    Standard Error (SE):The standard deviation of the sampling distribution. It measures the precision of the estimator.

    Shape:For large samples, the Central Limit Theorem tells us the sampling distribution tends to be **normal (bell-shaped)** even if the population is not normal.

    3. Key Concept: Standard Error (SE)

    The Standard Error is very important:

    \[ \text{Standard Error (SE)} = \sqrt{\text{Variance of the estimator}} \]

    • A small SE means the estimator doesn’t vary much from sample to sample — it’s more stable.
    • A large SE means the estimator can vary a lot — it’s less stable.

    Example:

    If we are estimating the sample mean \( \bar{x} \), the standard error is:

    \[ SE(\bar{x}) = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \]

    where:

    • \( \sigma \) is the population standard deviation,
    • \( n \) is the sample size.

    Thus, larger samples lead to smaller standard error (more precise estimates).

    Example

    Imagine you want to estimate the average height of college students:

  • You randomly select 100 students → Calculate the average height.
  • Repeat this process 1,000 times → Each time you get a slightly different average.

    • Plotting all these 1,000 averages gives you the sampling distribution of the sample mean!

    If the sample size is large, this distribution will look like a normal bell curve, even if individual heights are not perfectly normal.

    Relation to Model Evaluation

    In machine learning or statistical modeling:

  • Suppose you evaluate model accuracy based on one sample of data.
  • If you had different samples, your accuracy would be slightly different each time.
  • The sampling distribution of model accuracy tells you how stable your accuracy estimate is.

    • Thus, sampling distributions are key for understanding the uncertainty in model evaluation.

    Conclusion

    The sampling distribution of an estimator is the foundation for understanding uncertainty in any estimation process.

    It shows how much variability we should expect in our estimates when sampling randomly from the population.

    Mastering this concept helps us:

  • Build confidence intervals
  • Perform hypothesis tests
  • And make more reliable decisions based on sample data.

    • In statistical learning, understanding sampling distributions leads to more trustworthy and robust models.
